Most Common Adhesive Dispensing Problems in Production

Hot melt adhesive problems usually show up as missing beads, stringing, tailing, poor bond, charring, clogged nozzles, inconsistent bead size, bad placement, or material use that does not match expectations. The root cause is usually found by separating temperature, pressure, timing, nozzle condition, adhesive condition, and line speed.

Missing or Skipped Beads

Missing beads can come from trigger timing, gun response, clogged nozzles, low pressure, bad solenoid response, blocked filters, low tank level, or line-speed changes.

Troubleshoot Beads Check Timing

Stringing, Tailing, or Dripping

Stringing and dripping can point to temperature, viscosity, nozzle condition, gun shutoff, adhesive type, pressure, distance to part, or timing that keeps the gun on too long.

Troubleshooting Tool Parameter Guide

Poor Bond or Weak Adhesion

Poor bond can come from wrong adhesive temperature, contaminated material, open time, compression timing, bead size, substrate condition, product temperature, or not enough adhesive volume.

Bead Placement Is Off

Placement problems often come from trigger position, encoder scaling, line speed, product spacing, gun delay, sensor timing, or a reference point that is not understood clearly.

Nozzles Clog or Adhesive Chars

Charring and clogging can come from excessive temperature, long dwell time, old adhesive, dirty tank, contamination, blocked filters, poor maintenance, or adhesive sitting hot too long.

High adhesive usage can come from oversized bead, too much pressure, long on-time, wrong nozzle, poor pattern control, line-speed changes, rework, or unnecessary continuous application.

What People Commonly Misdiagnose in Adhesive Systems

Adhesive issues get misdiagnosed when every problem is treated like a temperature problem. Temperature matters, but so do pressure, timing, nozzle condition, adhesive age, line speed, product condition, and machine stabilization.

Changing Temperature Before Checking Timing

Bead placement and bead length problems are often timing or trigger-reference problems, not adhesive temperature problems. Check line speed and gun on-time before chasing temperature.

Ignoring Machine Warm-Up and Stabilization

Hot melt systems need time to stabilize. Judging bead quality too early can lead to unnecessary setting changes and inconsistent startup behavior.

Assuming Pressure Fixes Every Weak Bead

Higher pressure can increase flow, but it may also create stringing, overspray, waste, or poor cutoff. Bead size should match the part and process, not just look bigger.

Overlooking Nozzle and Filter Condition

A partially clogged nozzle or filter can make the system look like it has a parameter problem. Hardware condition should be checked before major process changes.

Forgetting About Adhesive Age and Contamination

Adhesive that has been overheated, contaminated, or left in the tank too long can behave differently even when the machine settings are unchanged.

Not Comparing Bead Assumptions to Real Production

Usage estimates depend on bead size, line speed, and cycle rate. If one assumption is wrong, material planning and cost estimates will be wrong too.
