PLC Troubleshooting Guide (Real-World Step-by-Step)

This guide walks through how to troubleshoot PLC systems in the real world — not just theory. Use this when a machine is down, acting inconsistently, or not behaving the way it should.

Rule #1: Always compare what the machine is physically doing vs what the PLC thinks is happening.
---

Step 1 – Is the PLC Running?

Before chasing anything else, confirm the PLC is actually operational.

  • Is the controller in RUN mode?
  • Any major faults present?
  • Are I/O modules powered?
  • Are status LEDs normal?

If the PLC is faulted or not running, nothing else matters yet.

---

Step 2 – Communication Problems

If HMIs, remote I/O, or devices aren’t communicating, isolate whether it's:

  • One device
  • Multiple devices
  • The entire network
  • Check IP addresses and subnet
  • Look for duplicate IP conflicts
  • Check Ethernet cables and switch ports
  • Verify PLC communication module status

Step 3 – Inputs (Sensors Not Working)

If the PLC isn’t seeing a signal:

  • Check voltage at the PLC input terminal
  • Check sensor output (not just power)
  • Verify sourcing vs sinking wiring
  • Watch input LED on the PLC

If the LED changes but logic doesn’t → programming issue If LED doesn’t change → wiring or device issue

---

Step 4 – Outputs (Actuators Not Responding)

  • Is the PLC output turning on?
  • Check voltage at output terminal
  • Check relay/contactor wiring
  • Verify field device power

Outputs fail more often due to wiring or field power than PLC logic.

---

Step 5 – Analog Signals

  • Is the raw signal correct?
  • Is scaling correct?
  • 4–20mA vs 0–10V mismatch?
Use Analog Scaling Calculator Check Voltage Drop
---

Step 6 – Missed Signals / Scan Time

Fast signals may not be captured by normal PLC scans.

  • Compare pulse duration to scan time
  • Use high-speed inputs if needed
  • Check input filters
Check Scan Time & Pulse Capture
---

Step 7 – Intermittent Problems

  • Loose wiring
  • Bad connectors
  • Voltage drops under load
  • Noise / grounding issues
  • Network instability

If it only happens sometimes, suspect: movement, vibration, or load changes

---

Final Advice

Most PLC problems are NOT programming problems.

  • ~70% wiring / electrical
  • ~20% configuration
  • ~10% logic
