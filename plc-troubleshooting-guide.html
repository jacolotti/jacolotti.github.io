Step-by-Step PLC Troubleshooting Process

Most PLC problems are not caused by the PLC itself. They are caused by signal issues, wiring problems, incorrect scaling, or timing limitations. This guide walks through a structured process to isolate the root cause.

Step 1: Verify Input Signal

Start by confirming that the input device is working and the signal is physically reaching the PLC. Check voltage, wiring, and sensor operation before looking at logic.

Step 2: Check Analog Scaling

Incorrect scaling is one of the most common issues. A signal may be correct electrically but displayed incorrectly due to conversion errors.

Use Analog Scaling Calculator

Step 3: Evaluate Scan Time & Missed Signals

If signals are short or fast, the PLC may not detect them due to scan time limitations.

Common PLC problems this guide helps diagnose

This guide is most useful when a PLC input is not behaving the way the machine should. In many cases, the PLC is not the real problem. The issue is usually one of four things: bad signal scaling, missed short-duration inputs, voltage loss in the field circuit, or unstable wiring.

  • PLC analog input reading wrong
  • 4–20 mA signal scaling wrong
  • PLC missing pulses or short signals
  • Sensor works sometimes but not always
  • Unexpected low voltage at field devices

Analog input troubleshooting

If an analog value looks wrong, start by checking whether the raw electrical signal is correct before changing logic. A properly wired 4–20 mA or voltage input can still display the wrong engineering value if the scaling is incorrect.

Analog Scaling Calculator

Missed pulses and scan-time issues

Short-duration signals can be lost if the PLC scan or input update behavior is too slow for the event being detected. This becomes more important with high-speed sensors, encoder-style signals, and fast machine cycles.

PLC Scan Time & Pulse Capture

Voltage drop and field wiring issues

Inputs and sensors can behave unpredictably when voltage at the device drops under load. Long cable runs, undersized conductors, weak terminations, and overloaded 24 VDC circuits all increase the chance of intermittent faults.
